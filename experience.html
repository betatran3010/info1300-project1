    Research Project with Professor Shimon Edelman

    • Position: Research Assistant
    • Time: Dec 2023–Dec 2024
    • Overview: Examined turn-taking timing variations in bilingual children.
    • Description: In this project, I worked with Professor Shimon Edelman and his Ph.D. student Vivian Zhang. This study aimed to explore whether constant linguistic juggling in bilingualism can potentially complicate the timing of turn-taking compared to monolingualism in infants. To do so, we used recordings of bilingual caregiver-infant conversations to analyze factors such as pause length, utterance initiation speed, and conversation flow to assess potential turn-taking delays. Although I mainly worked on the bilingualism project, I also contributed to the project investigating turn-taking timing in Italian infants with Down syndrome and autism spectrum disorder.
    • Responsibilities:
      • Annotated recordings of Italian, Mandarin, and English caregiver-infant conversations by coding onsets and offsets
      • Conducted literature reviews on turn-taking, word learning, caregiver feedback, attention, neuroscience, pragmatics, joint engagement, and computational frameworks
      • Calculated reliability coding scores using intraclass correlation coefficient (ICC) and Cohen’s kappa
      • Completed courses in Collaborative Institutional Training Initiative (CITI Program): Full Course on RCR for Social and Behavioral Sciences, IRB Training - Basic, IRB Training - Research with Children"
      • Trained new lab members on annotation and literature review procedures
    • Skills:
      • ELAN

    Independent Research Project with Dr. Lauren Grant

    • Position: Student Researcher
    • Time: Jun–Aug 2022
    • Overview: Produced an 11,000-word literature review on the effectiveness of technological interventions (computerized cognitive training, video games, and virtual reality) in preventing and mitigating episodic memory decline.
    • Description: Episodic memory is the type of memory that allows us to consciously recall past personal experiences and events in a determined temporal and spatial context. For people with Alzheimer's disease (AD) or mild cognitive impairment, lifestyle and activities of their daily living may be mildly or drastically impacted by the episodic memory decline. While traditional interventions like cognitive training may have proven effective, challenges such as social distancing, mobility issues, and limited healthcare access underscore the need for distance therapy or online-based interventions. The lack of ecological validity in traditional interventions may also require exploring other technological alternatives that improve this limitation. These findings inspired my research project on the effectiveness of technological cognitive-training interventions in slowing AD progression by mitigating the impact of its most prominent impairment—episodic memory decline. In this project, I received mentorship from Dr. Lauren Grant, a Postdoctoral Researcher from the Cognitive Control & Aging Laboratory at Washington University in St. Louis. Through this endeavor, I aspired to explore solutions to proactively augment cognitive function in preclinical or early-stage AD.
    • Responsibilities:
      • Produced an 11,000-word literature review on the effectiveness of technological interventions (computerized cognitive training, video games, and virtual reality) in preventing and mitigating episodic memory decline
      • Examined and referenced 184 scholarly sources about episodic memory, mild cognitive impairment, Alzheimer’s disease, and traditional and technological cognitive training methods
      • Learned about individual differences in aging and factors predicting episodic memory decline
    • Skills:
      • Literature review writing
